OUTER PLANETS

Mercury

Mercury can refer to the planet, the chemical element, or the Roman messenger god. The planet Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet of our solar system, a rocky, airless world with extreme temperature swings and a heavily cratered surface. The chemical element Mercury is a heavy, silvery-white metallic liquid at room temperature, known for its toxicity and historical use in thermometers.

Venus

Venus, the second planet from the Sun, is a rocky world with a thick, toxic atmosphere primarily composed of carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulfuric acid, making it the hottest planet in our solar system due to a runaway greenhouse effect. Its surface is marked by vast plains, highland regions, and numerous volcanoes, some of which may still be active. Venus has a slow rotation period, taking about 243 Earth days to complete one rotation, and it rotates in the opposite direction to most planets in the solar system.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and our home, a rocky world with a dynamic surface of land and water, known for being the only known planet to support life due to its unique atmosphere, liquid water, and stable temperatures. The planet has a solid inner core, a liquid outer core, and a flowing mantle that drives tectonic plates, creating mountains and valleys on its surface.

Mars

Mars, known as the "Red Planet" for its iron-rich rust-colored surface, is the fourth planet from the Sun. It's a cold, desert world with a thin carbon dioxide atmosphere, polar ice caps, volcanoes, and large canyons, including Olympus Mons, the solar system's largest volcano. Mars has weak gravity, seasons, and features like dust devils and global dust storms.

Jupiter

Jupiter is the largest planet in our Solar System and the fifth from the Sun, a gas giant primarily composed of hydrogen and helium. It's known for its distinctive colorful bands, powerful storms like the enduring Great Red Spot, and a massive magnetic field. Despite its enormous size—more than twice the mass of all other planets combined—Jupiter is thought to have a small rocky core deep within its gaseous layers

Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and is known for its stunning rings, which are made up of ice and rock particles. It is a gas giant with a thick atmosphere composed mainly of hydrogen and helium. Saturn has a low density, meaning it could float in water if a large enough body of water existed. The planet has at least 82 moons, with Titan being the largest and one of the most Earth-like bodies in the solar system.

Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and has a unique blue-green color due to methane in its atmosphere. It is an ice giant with a composition mainly of hydrogen, helium, and ices like water, ammonia, and methane. Uranus has a distinct tilt of about 98 degrees, causing extreme seasonal variations. It has a faint ring system and at least 27 known moons. The planet's atmosphere features strong winds and storms, making it an intriguing subject for study in our solar system.

Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and furthest known planet from the Sun, a dark, cold, and windy ice giant with a vibrant blue color due to methane in its atmosphere. It's the most massive of the ice giants, composed of a hot, dense fluid of water, ammonia, and methane surrounding a rocky core, but has no solid surface. Known for its supersonic winds, Neptune is the windiest planet in the solar system and is so far from the Sun that high noon there would seem like dim twilight.
